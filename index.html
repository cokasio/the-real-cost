05 — What Actually Worked

Calling Your Congressman Never Stopped a War.

Here's what did.

Montgomery, Alabama — 1955

Black people were 75% of bus riders. They stopped riding. For 381 days. The bus system lost $3,000 per day. They didn't ask permission. They built their own transportation — Black taxi drivers charged 10 cents, same as bus fare. The city went broke. Then they desegregated. Not because they grew a conscience — because they were losing money.

South Africa — 1960–1990

Students pressured 155 universities to divest. 26 states, 22 counties, 90+ cities banned doing business with South Africa. They didn't boycott grocery stores — they moved billions out of the economy. The regime collapsed. When Mandela was asked if sanctions helped: "Oh, there is no doubt."

South Korea — 2016–2017

15.87 million people showed up. Not once — every week for 20 weeks. In the cold. With candles. They didn't go home. Parliament said "public sentiment will not allow the president to return." Impeached 234–56. Constitutional Court upheld it 8–0. President removed.

Vietnam — 1965–1975

The war didn't end because of phone calls. It ended because soldiers started refusing orders. Thousands went AWOL. Draft resistance was so massive they couldn't prosecute everyone. Veterans Against the War became what Nixon's chief of staff called "the most dangerous organization in America." When the people fighting the war say it's wrong — that's what breaks through.

None of these movements asked nicely. They made it impossible to continue business as usual. They cost the powerful money, credibility, or the ability to function. That's the only language power has ever understood.

3.5% of Americans = 11.5 million people. South Korea got 15.87 million.
