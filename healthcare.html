06 — The Hidden Scams

The Games They Play With Your Life.

The Medical Loss Ratio Trick

The ACA says insurers must spend 80–85% of premiums on actual care. Sounds good. Here's how they gamed it:

Profit is capped as a percentage. So the only way to increase absolute profit is to let total costs rise. 15% of $1,000 = $150. 15% of $2,000 = $300. Higher healthcare costs = higher insurance profits.

CVS/Caremark fills a prescription at its own pharmacy: records a $17,710 "medical claim" for a drug that costs $70 to acquire. Counted as "medical spending" — not profit.

Source: FREOPP →

The Revolving Door

9 out of 10 FDA commissioners went to work for pharmaceutical companies after leaving office (2006–2019). The people who regulate your drugs leave to get paid by the companies they just regulated.

In 2025, after HHS layoffs, hundreds of ex-federal health workers immediately took jobs at Eli Lilly, Pfizer, Merck, Roche, and Novartis.

57% of departing FDA oncology reviewers took biopharma industry jobs.

Source: STAT News / BMJ →

The Prior Auth Death Toll

1 in 3 doctors has personally witnessed prior authorization cause a serious adverse event — hospitalization, permanent injury, or death.

A 58-year-old cancer patient in West Virginia. His doctor ordered proton beam therapy. The insurer denied it — "not medically necessary." By the time they reversed the denial, it was too late. He died.

78% of doctors say patients abandon recommended treatment because the insurer said no. Not because of medicine. Because of paperwork.

An insurance company employee — not a doctor — overrode his physician's treatment plan without examining him. That is, by definition, practicing medicine without a license.

07 — What You Can Do

They Bet You Won't Fight. Prove Them Wrong.

01

Appeal Every Denial. Every Single One.

60–80% of denials are overturned on appeal. The insurance company is counting on you giving up. Don't. Request the specific clinical criteria they used to deny you. Ask your doctor to write a peer-to-peer review request. If your state has an external review process, use it — it's free and binding.

02

File With Your State Insurance Commissioner

Every state has one. They investigate complaints. When enough complaints stack up against an insurer, regulators act. Your complaint isn't just for you — it builds the case for everyone. Find yours here →
